The Sounds of English

The phonemic chart below contains the 44 sounds of (British) spoken English.

The chart is divided into vowels and consonants. The vowels are further divided into single vowels and diphthongs. Single vowels contain one sound. Diphthongs are vowels which are made up of two sounds.

The consonants are divided into voiced and unvoiced consonants. For example, P and B are produced in the same place in the mouth with the tongue in the same position. The only difference is that P is an unvoiced sound (no vibration of the vocal cords) while B is a voiced sound (vocal cords vibrate).

Word Stress

In English, each syllable in a word is not said with the same emphasis. Each word has one stressed syllable. The other syllables are unstressed or weak.

Take the word conversation, for example. Conversation has four syllables. The stress in on the third syllable.

Stressed syllables are longer, louder, and they are pronounced more clearly than unstressed syllables.

Intonation

Try listening to someone talking without focusing to the words: the melody you can hear is intonation.

Intonation is about how we say things, rather than what we say. Without intonation, it's impossible to understand the expressions and thoughts that go with words.

There are some general rules we can follow to learn some typical intonation patterns. Look at the examples below and listen to the audio to see if you can hear the patterns.

Rising Intonation

Firstly, when we ask a yes/no question, we tend to use rising intonation.

Falling Intonation

Secondly, wh-questions are typically accompanied by falling intonation.

Combination Intonation

Finally, when we list things, each item on the list has rising intonation except for the final item which has falling intonation.

Recording your voice and analysing your pronunciation!

When you go to the practise section, you can choose which area of pronunciation you would like to practise - indvidual sounds, word stress, or intonation.

After you have chosen an area, you can choose a word or phrase from the dropdown menu.

When you've chosen a word, use the buttons to record yourself saying the word.

You can listen to your recording, and record again if you're not happy with it.

Once you are satisfied with the recording, click to analyse your pronunciation.

Feedback

Understanding the feedback on your pronunciation is essential for you to be able to improve. The different kinds of feedback given are explored below.

Sounds

If you have chosen Individual Sounds, you will be able to see all of the sounds contained in the word. You can click on each sound and see how to pronounce it and listen to other words containing that sound.

The system will either say that your pronunciation was excellent (see below), advise you on which sound to practise, or if your pronunciation was not clear it will ask that you go back and try again.

You can then click on the individual sounds contained in the word to find out more about each sound.

Word Stress

If you have chosen Word Stress, you will be able to see the stress pattern of the word represented in a diagram.

The system will tell you on which syllable to placed the main stress. You can compare this to the model pronunciation.

Intonation Pattern

If you have chosen Intonation, you will be able to see the intonation pattern of the phrase. It will be represented using rising or falling arrows.

The system will show you the pitch pattern of your pronunciation, and you can compare this to the model. You can identify if your pitch is rising or falling.

About

Artificial Intelligibility is an MSc Project which aims to explore efficient ways to detect errors in pronunciation and provide feedback using speech technology.

The main objectives are to explore the kinds of visual feedback users find effective, and to investigate how speech technologies can be employed in order to accurately detect both segmental and prosodic errors in pronunciation.
